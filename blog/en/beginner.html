Spider Solitaire for Beginners
— Easy Step-by-Step Guide

If you've just discovered Spider Solitaire and aren't sure where to start, you're in the right place. This beginner's guide explains everything in plain language — no card game jargon, no assumed knowledge. By the end, you'll understand the goal, know how to make your first moves, and have a clear path to winning your first game. The best part: Spider Solitaire Pro includes an AI coach that will help you every step of the way.

Start at 1 Suit — Always

Spider Solitaire comes in three difficulty levels: 1 Suit, 2 Suits, and 4 Suits. As a beginner, always start at 1 Suit (Easy). Here's why:

  • All cards are the same suit (♠), so you don't need to worry about mixing suits
  • Any sequence of descending cards can be moved as a group
  • The game is significantly more manageable, letting you focus on learning the core mechanics
  • Even experienced players enjoy 1-Suit for a relaxing, winnable game

Once you've won at 1 Suit a few times and understand the rhythm of the game, you can move up to 2 Suits. 4 Suits is genuinely challenging — many players play for years before winning at that level.

How to set difficulty in Spider Solitaire Pro

At the top of the screen, you'll see three buttons labeled 1♠, 2♠, and 4♠. Tap 1♠ to select 1-Suit mode. The selected difficulty is shown in gold. You can change difficulty at any time to start a new game.

Understanding the Goal in Simple Terms

Here's the goal of Spider Solitaire in one sentence: arrange all the cards into 8 complete sets, each going from King to Ace in the same suit.

A "complete set" means having K, Q, J, 10, 9, 8, 7, 6, 5, 4, 3, 2, A all in one column, all the same suit. When that happens, those 13 cards automatically disappear from the board and move to the completion area at the top. Do that 8 times and you win.

The board has 10 columns with cards stacked face-down (you can't see them) and face-up (you can see and move them). Your job is to gradually uncover all the hidden cards and organize everything into those 8 complete sets.

Your First 5 Moves: A Walkthrough

When you start a new game, here's a simple routine to follow for your first few moves:

1

Look at all 10 columns

Take a moment to see which face-up cards are showing. Don't rush — Spider Solitaire rewards observation.

2

Find a move that flips a hidden card

If moving a card would make the card below it flip face-up, that's usually a great move. Hidden cards are your biggest unknown — uncover them as fast as possible.

3

Build sequences going downward

Place lower-ranking cards on top of higher-ranking cards (e.g., put a 5 on a 6). This builds the sequences you need to complete the game.

4

If you're stuck, tap 💡 Hint

The Hint button highlights every card you can currently move. It's free to use and has no penalty. Use it whenever you can't spot a move.

5

Don't deal from stock until you have to

The stack of cards in the top-left corner is your stock pile. You'll use it eventually, but try to exhaust your current moves first. Dealing too early can create problems.

Key Concepts Explained Simply

Face-Down vs Face-Up Cards

Cards showing their back (face-down) cannot be seen or moved. Cards showing their face (face-up) can be moved. When you move a face-up card away from the bottom of a stack, the card below it flips face-up. Uncovering hidden cards is one of your main goals throughout the game.

The Stock Pile

In the top-left corner, you'll see a stack of cards. This is the stock pile. When you tap it, one card is added to each of the 10 columns. You can do this 5 times total. Important: you can't deal from the stock if any column is empty — fill the empty column first.

Empty Columns

If you move all the cards out of a column, it becomes empty. Empty columns are valuable — you can temporarily store any card there, which helps you rearrange other columns. Don't fill them carelessly.

Moving Multiple Cards

In 1-Suit mode, if you have a sequence like 7, 6, 5, 4 stacked in order in a column, you can move all 4 cards at once onto an 8. This is one of the things that makes 1-Suit mode much easier than higher difficulties.

Common Beginner Mistakes (and How to Fix Them)

Mistake 1: Moving cards without thinking

New players often move the first card they notice without considering whether it's a good move. Instead, scan the whole board before each move. Ask: "Will this move flip a hidden card? Will it help me build a sequence?"

Mistake 2: Dealing from stock too early

When you can't immediately see a move, it's tempting to deal from stock. But usually there are valid moves you haven't noticed yet. Use the 💡 Hint button first — if no moves are highlighted, then it's a good time to deal.

Mistake 3: Filling empty columns randomly

When a column opens up, don't rush to fill it with just any card. Empty columns are a resource. Only move something into an empty column when you have a specific reason — like needing to temporarily store a card to reorganize another column.

Mistake 4: Not using Undo

Many beginners forget that Undo is available and unlimited. If a move makes things worse, go back and try something different. There's no penalty for using Undo. It's one of the most important tools for learning the game.

Mistake 5: Giving up too early

Spider Solitaire can look hopeless when the board is messy, but there's often a path through. Before giving up, try the AI coach — it might see a route you missed. The game is not over until there are literally no valid moves and the stock is empty.

Building Good Habits from Day One

The best way to improve at Spider Solitaire is to build a few consistent habits early on. These will carry you from beginner to intermediate:

  1. Scan the whole board before each move — don't just react to the first move you see
  2. Always ask "does this flip a hidden card?" — if yes, it's almost certainly a good move
  3. Use Hint when stuck, Undo when wrong — both tools are there to help, use them
  4. Think of the stock as a last resort — exhaust your options before dealing
  5. Play the Daily Puzzle — every day you get a fresh board, and scores reset so you can always start fresh

Even playing just a few games a day, you'll find yourself spotting opportunities faster and winning more consistently within a couple of weeks.

What's Next: Moving Up to 2 Suits

Once you've won at 1 Suit several times and feel comfortable with the flow of the game, try 2 Suits. The key difference is that cards won't automatically form a movable group unless they're the same suit. You'll need to think more carefully about which cards to stack together.

The same principles apply — uncover face-down cards, protect empty columns, don't deal too early — but the board is much less forgiving. Don't be discouraged if it takes many attempts to win your first 2-Suit game. That's completely normal.
