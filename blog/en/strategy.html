Spider Solitaire Strategy Tips
— How to Win More

Spider Solitaire has a deceptively low win rate — even experienced players can struggle without a clear strategy. The difference between winning and losing usually comes down to a handful of core principles: how you manage empty columns, whether you prioritize same-suit building, and when you choose to deal from the stock. This guide breaks down proven strategies that will immediately raise your win rate, from foundational habits to advanced techniques.

The #1 Priority: Uncover Face-Down Cards

Every face-down card is a locked possibility. You don't know what's underneath, which means you can't plan around it. The single most impactful habit you can develop is to prioritize moves that flip face-down cards face-up.

When you have multiple valid moves available, ask yourself: which move will reveal a hidden card? If one option flips a card and another doesn't, almost always choose the one that flips.

More face-up cards means more options, more flexibility, and more information to work with. Columns with many face-down cards are your bottlenecks — target them first.

Quick check: Before each move, scan all columns and count how many face-down cards are in each one. Focus your energy on the columns with the most hidden cards.

Always Build Same-Suit Sequences

This is perhaps the most important strategic principle in Spider Solitaire: whenever you have a choice, place cards onto same-suit cards rather than different-suit cards.

Here's why it matters so much: only pure same-suit sequences (e.g., 7♠ 6♠ 5♠) can be moved as a group. Mixed-suit stacks (e.g., 7♠ 6♥ 5♠) can only be moved one card at a time. This dramatically limits your flexibility in later stages of the game.

In 2-Suit and 4-Suit games, mixed stacks are unavoidable — but you should always treat them as temporary arrangements. The moment you can "clean up" a mixed stack by rebuilding it in the same suit, do it.

Same-Suit Building in Practice

  • If a 7♥ can go on either an 8♥ or an 8♠, always choose the 8♥
  • When reorganizing a column, try to leave the same-suit cards on top
  • Think of mixed-suit stacks as "locked" — invest moves to unlock them

Master the Empty Column

An empty column is one of the most powerful resources in the game — and one of the most easily wasted. Treat every empty column as a precious asset, not a place to dump cards.

What Empty Columns Are For

Empty columns allow you to temporarily store card sequences while you reorganize other columns. Think of them as free storage buffers. Without them, many column rearrangements are simply impossible.

Common Mistake: Filling Empty Columns Too Early

A very common mistake is to immediately fill an empty column with a King or a random card sequence as soon as the column opens up. Resist this urge. Only move something into an empty column when it's part of a deliberate plan — for example, to free up space in another column that has a useful card buried under a stack.

How Many Empty Columns Do You Need?

As a rough guide:

  • 1 empty column lets you do basic rearrangements
  • 2 empty columns give you substantial freedom to reorganize
  • 3+ empty columns means you're in excellent shape — almost certainly winning
Advanced tip: In 4-Suit mode, creating a second empty column is often the turning point of the game. If you can create two empty columns simultaneously, your win probability jumps dramatically.

Think Before You Deal from Stock

Dealing from the stock pile adds 10 new cards to the board — one per column. It can open up new possibilities, but it also buries face-up cards under new face-down cards, potentially undoing progress you've made.

Never deal from stock just because you can't see a move. Instead, use the Hint button (💡) to check if there are moves you've missed. Dealing prematurely is one of the most common ways to put yourself in an unwinnable position.

When to Deal from Stock

  • You have genuinely exhausted all useful moves in the current tableau
  • You have no empty columns (otherwise, free up a column first)
  • You've already built as many same-suit sequences as possible
  • Your face-down card count is low — so new cards won't bury too much progress

Preparing for a Deal

Before dealing, try to create at least one empty column. After the deal, you'll immediately have more flexibility to work with the new cards. Dealing onto a completely packed board without any empty columns is usually a sign that the game is in a difficult state.

Use Undo Without Guilt

Spider Solitaire Pro offers unlimited Undo — and you should use it freely, especially while developing your strategy. Undo is not cheating; it's a learning tool.

When a move leads to a worse board state than you expected, undo it and try something different. Over time, this trial-and-error process builds intuition. You'll start to recognize patterns and anticipate consequences without needing to undo as often.

If you find yourself undoing the same move repeatedly, that's a signal to step back and look at the whole board — you may be stuck in a local trap that requires a completely different approach from several moves back.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

These are the strategic errors that most often lead to losing positions:

1. Moving cards without a plan

Moving a card just because you can is rarely good strategy. Every move should serve a purpose — flipping a card, building a same-suit sequence, or freeing up an important card.

2. Wasting empty columns

Filling an empty column with a card that doesn't advance your position is a common and costly mistake. Once filled, that column is no longer a resource.

3. Ignoring blocked columns

If one column has 8 face-down cards and another has 2, you should almost always prioritize the column with 8 — the sooner you unblock it, the better your options become in the mid-game.

4. Dealing too early

Dealing from stock when there are still productive moves in the tableau reduces your control over the board. Always squeeze as much value as possible before dealing.

5. Playing too fast

Spider Solitaire rewards patience. Slowing down, scanning all columns before each move, and thinking two or three moves ahead will produce dramatically better results than fast, instinctive play.

Advanced Technique: Sequence Splitting

Once you're comfortable with the basics, try "sequence splitting" — deliberately breaking apart a long mixed-suit stack using empty columns to rebuild it in the correct same-suit order.

For example, if you have a stack of 8♠ 7♥ 6♠ 5♥, you can use two empty columns to reorder the cards so that the spades and hearts form separate same-suit sequences. This costs several moves and uses your precious empty columns, but the payoff — being able to move large groups of cards at once — is often worth it.

This technique is most powerful in the mid-game, before you've dealt all your stock cards, when there's still enough flexibility to recover from temporary setbacks.
